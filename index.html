How does it work? It just werks.

If you get paid exactly the same ammount every period for, like, ever, each period you will get less and less, in present value, like so:

\[PV= \frac{C}{1+r} + \frac{C}{(1+i)^2} + \frac{C}{(1+i)^3} + ... + \frac{C}{{(1+i)}^{n}}\]

Can also be written as \[\sum_{k=1}^{\infty }\frac{C}{(1+i)^{k}}\]

As we all learned (right?), the sum of a geometric series is given by... ok, we can prove it real quick. Let's call $\frac{1}{1+i}$ simply $r$ and . Hence, we have \[\sum_{k=1}^{\infty }Cr^{k}\] Let's expand this. \[\sum_{k=1}^{\infty }Cr^{k}=Cr + Cr^2 + Cr^3 + ... + Cr^n\] Now, again, for simplification purposes, let's give the letter $S$ to $\sum_{k=1}^{\infty }Cr^{k}$ \[S=Cr + Cr^2 + Cr^3 + ... + Cr^n\] Now, let's get busy. We'll start by multiplying by $r$ on both sides. \[S=Cr+Cr^2+Cr^3+...+Cr^{n}\Leftrightarrow Sr=Cr^2+Cr^3+...+Cr^{n+1}\] Let's subtract $S$ on both sides. Because $S$ equals the sum of that lovely Geometric progression (Don't you love series? Me neither.), we get this: \[Sr-S=\left [Cr^2+Cr^3+...+Cr^{n+1} \right ]-\left [Cr+Cr^2+...+Cr^{n} \right ]\Leftrightarrow\] \[S(r-1)=Cr^{n+1}-Cr\] Do you see where we are getting at? We're close. \[S(r-1)=Cr^{n+1}-Cr\Leftrightarrow S(r-1)=C(r^{n+1}-r)\Leftrightarrow S=C\frac{r^{n+1}-r}{r-1}\Leftrightarrow -S=C\frac{r^{n+1}-r}{1-r}\] \[\mid r \mid=\left |\frac{1}{1+i} \right | < 1, \: so, \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} Cr^{n+1}=0\] \[-S=\frac{-Cr}{1-r}\Leftrightarrow S=\frac{Cr}{1-r}=\frac{C\times \frac{1}{1+i}}{1-\frac{1}{1+i}}\] Hang in there! \[\frac{C\frac{1}{1+i}}{\frac{1+i-1}{1+i}}\Leftrightarrow \frac{\frac{C}{1+i}}{\frac{i}{1+i}}\Leftrightarrow \frac{C}{i}\] Ta-freaking-Dah!

\[PV=\frac{C}{i}\times \left ( 1-\frac{1}{{(1+i)}^{n}} \right )\]

Remember how we (I did, anyway) proved the formula for the perpetuity? If not, check it out. If yes, the idea is the same, but this time, instead of an infinite number of periods, the number of periods is finite. \[\sum_{k=1}^{n}Cr^{k},\: r=\frac{1}{1+i}\]
For now, it's the same as always. Let's expand again. \[\sum_{k=1}^{n}Cr^{k}=Cr + Cr^2 + Cr^3 + ... + Cr^n\] And now let's replace that sigma by $S$ again and multiply by $r$ on both sides. \[S=Cr+Cr^2+Cr^3+...+Cr^{n}\Leftrightarrow Sr=Cr^2+Cr^3+...+Cr^{n+1}\] Now we'll subtract $S$ on both sides. \[Sr-S=\left [Cr^2+Cr^3+...+Cr^{n+1} \right ]-\left [Cr+Cr^2+...+Cr^{n} \right ]\Leftrightarrow\] \[S(r-1)=Cr^{n+1}-Cr\Leftrightarrow S(1-r)=Cr-Cr^{n+1}\] But, Araragi-kun, what the hell are you doing? You are doing the same thing you did with Perpetuity, baka!

I know, I know. Wait a bit. With the Perpetuity, we used the limit of ${Cr}^{n}$ then $n$ approaches $\infty$, which is 0. Here, $n$ does not approach infinity, as it is a finite number, as I said. \[S=\frac{Cr-Cr^{n+1}}{1-r}\Leftrightarrow S=C\frac{r-r^{n+1}}{1-r}\Leftrightarrow S=C\frac{\frac{1}{1+i}-(\frac{1}{1+i})^{n+1}}{\frac{1+i}{1+i}-\frac{1}{1+i}}=\frac{C}{i}\frac{\frac{1}{1+i}-(\frac{1}{1+i})^{n+1}}{\frac{1}{1+i}}\] Whoa, what a mess. \[S=\frac{C}{i}\frac{\frac{1}{1+i}-(\frac{1}{1+i})^{n+1}}{\frac{1}{1+i}}\Leftrightarrow S=\frac{C}{i}\left ( 1-\left (\frac{1}{1+i} \right )^n \right )\] $1^n=1$ \[PV=\frac{C}{i}\left (1-\frac{1}{(1+i)^n} \right )\] Yay.

Financial Ratios

You should already know this from Financial Accounting.

Time value of money

This is pretty simple. $100\$$ today is not $100\$$ tomorrow, it's probably less, depending on the discount rate, the rate at which money loses its value.

How much time does it take to double an investment?

$C = capital, \, r = interest\, rate,\, t=units\,of\,time\,(may\, be\, years)$

\[C\times (1+r)^t=2C\Leftrightarrow (1+r)^t=2\Leftrightarrow ln((1+r)^t)=ln(2)\Leftrightarrow\] \[t\times ln(1+r)=ln(2)\Leftrightarrow t=\frac{ln(2)}{ln(1+r)}\] So, if $r=5\%$, it takes $t=\frac{ln(2)}{ln(1+r)}\Leftrightarrow t=\frac{ln(2)}{ln(1.05)}\approx 14.2\, years$.

Difference between interest rates

Imagine that you have a 10% annual nominal rate and it compounds quarterly (every 3 months). You have an interest rate of $10/4=2.5\%$ per quarter. What is the effective annual interest rate? It's not 10%, it will be $(1+2.5\%)^4-1\approx 10.38\%$, which is greater that 10%. This means that investing at an annual rate of 10.38% compounded annually is the same as investing at 10% compounded quaterly. This is very important when dealing with spot rates, where you will, invariably, fail.

Bonds, James Bonds

At discount, not premium

What is a bond?

When you buy a bond (obrigações), you are buying part of a company's debt. Why would you buy a company's debt? Because they can give you a nice return. There are companies that rank the bonds (you may have heard of Moody's or Standard & Poor's) and insure (you may have also heard of CDS or interest rate swaps) debt.

Characteristics of a bond

  • Maturity date - the ammount of time till the end of the bond. If you buy a bond with 10-year bond 5 years after being issued, the time to maturity is only 5 years and you will only receive interest during those 5 years.
  • Coupon frequency - period of the coupons. Usually annually (once a year) or semi-annually (every 6 months). Payments are equal each period.
  • Coupon rate - the interest
  • Face value - the value that will be returned at maturity and over which you receive interest

How to value a bond

The value of a bond is determined by present value of coupon payments and face value (the expected future CF), so you just need to use the Annuity or Perpetuity equation. You just need the Coupon rate, maturity date and yield-to-maturity (usually the Effective Annual Rate). \[Bond\: Value = \frac{Coupon}{YTM}\left ( 1-\frac{1}{(1+YTM)^t} \right )+\frac{Face\,Value}{(1+y)^t}\] It's a no-brainer, really.

Imagine that you have a bond 5.5 years to maturity, face value of 1000, coupon rate of 5% paid semi-annually and the best alternative has an annual yield of 2%.

Coupon = $5\%*1000/2$ = 25 \[Bond\: Value = \frac{25}{0.02}\left ( 1-\frac{1}{(1.02)^{5.5}} \right )+\frac{1000}{(1.02)^{5.5}}\approx 1025.80\] But, Araragi-kun, why don't you divide 2% by 2? Isn't it semi-annual?

Good question. Yes, it is. But we are dealing with an effective annual rate that was magically given to us, so we just needed to raise it to the number of years, 5.5. If you divide 2% by 2, you just get half of the annual yield and a big mess in your hands.

But Araragi-kun, couldn't we transform the EAR into an equivalent semi-annual rate and raise it to the number of semesters instead?

We could. But it would be even more tiring and time consuming that formulating this question. And time is money, don't waste it.
